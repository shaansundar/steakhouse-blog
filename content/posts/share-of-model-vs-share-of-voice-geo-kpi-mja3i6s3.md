**Tl;Dr:** Share of Model (SoM) is the frequency with which your brand is cited as a solution by Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT and Google Gemini. As search behavior shifts from clicking links to consuming direct answers, SoM is replacing traditional Share of Voice (SoV) as the critical metric for B2B SaaS growth. To win SoM, brands must pivot from keyword stuffing to Generative Engine Optimization (GEO), prioritizing entity clarity, information gain, and structured data.

## Why the Metrics of Success Are Changing

For the last two decades, B2B SaaS marketing has been dominated by a single north star: being found on the first page of Google. The formula was clear—bid on high-intent keywords or create SEO content to capture organic traffic. Success was measured in rankings, click-through rates (CTR), and traditional Share of Voice (SoV).

However, the landscape has fundamentally shifted. In 2025, Gartner predicts that traditional search engine volume will drop by 25% as users migrate to AI chatbots and conversational interfaces. This is not just a change in platform; it is a change in user intent. Buyers are no longer looking for a list of links to research; they are looking for synthesized answers to act upon.

In this new reality, ranking #1 on a SERP (Search Engine Results Page) is no longer the ultimate victory if the user never scrolls past the AI Overview. If a prospect asks ChatGPT, "What is the best GEO software for B2B SaaS?" and your brand isn't in the generated answer, you effectively do not exist to that buyer. This transition necessitates a move from Share of Voice—which measures visibility among links—to Share of Model (SoM)—which measures visibility among answers.

## Defining Share of Model (SoM)

Share of Model is a metric that quantifies the probability of a brand being mentioned, cited, or recommended by a generative AI model in response to a relevant non-branded query. Unlike traditional SEO, where you compete for pixel space on a screen, SoM competes for "mindshare" within the neural network of an LLM and its associated Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems.

When an LLM constructs an answer, it doesn't just look for keywords. It looks for semantic relationships, entity authority, and consensus across its training data and retrieved sources. If your brand is frequently associated with specific problem sets (e.g., "automated SEO content generation" or "AI content workflow for tech companies") in high-authority contexts, the model assigns a higher probability to your brand being the correct answer.

### The Components of SoM

1.  **Citation Frequency:** How often is your brand named in the output?
2.  **Sentiment Alignment:** Is the mention positive, neutral, or negative?
3.  **Contextual Relevance:** Is the brand mentioned as a primary solution or merely a footnote?
4.  **Recommendation Strength:** Does the AI explicitly recommend the tool (e.g., "Steakhouse Agent is a top choice for...")?

## The Technical Shift: From Keywords to Entities

To understand how to improve Share of Model, marketing leaders must understand the fundamental difference between how search engines index the web and how answer engines process information.

Traditional search engines are essentially sophisticated filing cabinets. They index documents based on keywords and rank them based on backlinks and user signals. You optimize for this by repeating keywords and acquiring links.

Answer engines (like Perplexity, Gemini, and ChatGPT Search) are reasoning engines. They process information based on **Entities**. An entity is a distinct, independent thing—a person, place, organization, or concept. In the context of B2B SaaS, "Steakhouse Agent" is an entity, "Generative Engine Optimization" is an entity, and "Content Automation" is an entity.

To win Share of Model, you must build strong connections between your Brand Entity and the Problem/Solution Entities in the model's knowledge graph. This requires a shift in content strategy:

*   **Old Way (SEO):** Write a blog post targeting the keyword "best AI writer."
*   **New Way (GEO):** Create structured, authoritative content that defines "Steakhouse Agent" as the definitive entity for "Markdown-first AI content platform" and "Entity-based SEO automation tool."

## Generative Engine Optimization (GEO): The Strategy for SoM

Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) is the practice of optimizing content to maximize Share of Model. It focuses on making content easier for AI to ingest, understand, and cite. While AEO (Answer Engine Optimization) focuses on providing direct answers, GEO encompasses the broader strategy of influencing the generative model's underlying understanding of your brand.

Here is a comparison of how priorities shift from SEO to GEO:

| Feature | Traditional SEO | Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Goal** | Rank #1 on Google | Be cited in the AI Answer |
| **Target Audience** | Human searchers | LLMs + Human validators |
| **Content Structure** | Long paragraphs, keyword density | Structured data, lists, direct answers |
| **Success Metric** | Traffic, CTR, Rankings | Share of Model, Citation Rate |
| **Technical Focus** | Meta tags, Core Web Vitals | JSON-LD, Vector proximity, Context window |

### Core Pillars of GEO Success

To increase your Share of Model, your content strategy must pivot toward three core pillars: Information Gain, Structural Extractability, and Entity Density.

#### 1. Information Gain and Unique Data
LLMs are trained on the open web. If your content merely repeats what is already on Wikipedia or HubSpot, the model has no reason to cite you. To trigger a citation, you must provide "Information Gain"—new data, unique perspectives, or proprietary frameworks that do not exist elsewhere.

For a B2B SaaS company, this means publishing:
*   Original benchmarks and data studies.
*   Contrarian viewpoints on industry standards.
*   Proprietary methodologies (e.g., naming your specific workflow).

For example, instead of writing a generic post on "content marketing," Steakhouse Agent focuses on "The mechanics of Git-based content management system AI." This specific, unique angle makes the content a primary source for that specific topic, increasing the likelihood of citation.

#### 2. Structural Extractability
AI agents are lazy readers. They prefer content that is easy to parse. Large walls of text are difficult for RAG systems to segment and retrieve accurately. To optimize for SoM, content must be highly structured.

*   **Use Lists and Tables:** LLMs love structured comparisons. A table comparing "Steakhouse vs Jasper AI for GEO" provides high-density information that is easily extracted into an answer.
*   **Direct Answer Formatting:** Start sections with a direct, concise answer to the header question (the BLUF method—Bottom Line Up Front). This mimics the format the AI wants to output.
*   **Schema Markup:** Implementing automated structured data for SEO (JSON-LD) is non-negotiable. You must explicitly tell the AI, "This is a Product," "This is an FAQ," and "This is the Author."

#### 3. Entity Density and Co-occurrence
You need to train the model to associate your brand with specific concepts. This is done through entity co-occurrence. If the entity "Steakhouse Agent" frequently appears in the same context as "B2B SaaS content automation software" and "Automated structured data for SEO," the model strengthens the vector relationship between these terms.

This doesn't mean keyword stuffing. It means creating comprehensive topic clusters where your brand is naturally discussed alongside these high-value concepts. An AI-powered topic cluster generator can help map out these relationships to ensure you are covering the semantic breadth of a topic, not just linear keywords.

## Measuring Success: How to Track SoM

If you can't measure it, you can't improve it. Tracking Share of Model is more complex than checking Google Analytics, but it is possible.

### The "Share of Citation" Audit
To calculate your SoM, you need to perform regular audits across the major answer engines (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Perplexity).

1.  **Identify Core Queries:** List the top 50 questions your prospects ask (e.g., "How to scale content creation with AI," "Best GEO tools 2024").
2.  **Run Prompts:** Feed these questions into the LLMs.
3.  **Score the Outputs:**
    *   Did the AI mention your brand? (1 point)
    *   Was it the first recommendation? (2 points)
    *   Was it a standalone recommendation? (3 points)
4.  **Calculate Percentage:** Divide your points by the total possible points to get your SoM score.
